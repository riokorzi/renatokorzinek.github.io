PEM Fuel Cell Design

Led the design and manufacturing of a custom hydrogen fuel cell. Focused on optimizing flow channels, testing hydrogen sensors, and benchmarking performance.

CAD Design 3D Printing Electrochemistry MATLAB

Project Overview

This project involved the complete design and fabrication of a Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) fuel cell from scratch. The goal was to understand the fundamental principles of fuel cell operation while achieving competitive performance metrics compared to commercial units.

Technical Approach

• Designed custom bipolar plates with serpentine flow channels using CAD software
• Optimized channel geometry for uniform reactant distribution and water management
• Integrated hydrogen leak detection sensors for safety monitoring
• Conducted extensive performance testing under various operating conditions
• Benchmarked results against commercial PEM fuel cell units

Results & Impact

Achieved 85% efficiency compared to commercial baseline, with significant cost reduction through additive manufacturing techniques. The project demonstrated feasibility of rapid prototyping for fuel cell development and provided insights into scaling considerations.

Second-Order Dynamic System Analysis

Advanced signal processing and system identification using linear potentiometer measurements to analyze dynamic behavior and validate theoretical models.

Signal Processing MATLAB/Simulink Data Analysis Control Theory

Project Overview

This project focused on the experimental characterization and theoretical modeling of a second-order dynamic system. Using precision instrumentation and advanced signal processing techniques, we achieved exceptional correlation between experimental data and mathematical models.

Methodology

• Implemented high-precision linear potentiometer for displacement measurement
• Developed custom signal conditioning and filtering algorithms
• Applied system identification techniques to extract system parameters
• Validated results through frequency domain analysis and step response testing
• Achieved sub-5% error between experimental and theoretical predictions

Key Findings

Successfully identified natural frequency, damping ratio, and system gain with high accuracy. The methodology developed can be applied to various mechanical and aerospace systems for real-time monitoring and control applications.

Coastal Surveillance Drone Project

UAV Prototyping Project Design Soldering Drone Programming Drone Assembly GPS and Video Systems

Project Scope

During my high school career, I had the pleasure of interning at Seaside Sustainability, a local non-profit organization focusing on coastal conservation. There, I developed my own project building a high-efficiency long range drone to survey coastlines and collect data on fishing pollution in salt marshes on Cape Ann. This data was used to provide evidence to mitigate plastics from fishing pollution in the area. The project was designed and executed by myself with partnership between St. John's Preparatory School and Seaside Sustainability. One personal goal of mine was to build a quadcopter capable of outperforming the DJI drones of the time. By using larger low kv motors, efficient electrical components, and strategic weight placement, my drone reached double the DJI flight time of 26 min at only half the cost.

Gasket Project

• we were asked to accurately measure and sketch a metal block in order to produce a gasket
• each block had slightly different dimensions, therefore accuracy in measurement was key
• also required to make CAD models in Solidworks of our sketches and then learned how to use Gibbscam to produce a gcode file to send to a CNC mill at BU EPIC
• overall I am satisfied with my project however I misunderstood one step in the instructions for Gibbscam and therefore my two pin holes are too large
In the assigned Gasket Project, our task was to precisely measure and sketch a metal block, emphasizing the accuracy due to slight variations in the dimensions of each block. This meticulous approach was crucial as the end goal was to manufacture a gasket tailored to the specific dimensions of the metal block. Following the measurement and sketching phase, we transitioned to Solidworks to create detailed CAD models, ensuring the accurate representation of the metal block. With CAD the model, we learned and utilized Gibbscam, a pivotal step in the project, where I encountered a misunderstanding in the instructions. My misunderstanding caused me to make the pin hole cuts outer cuts instead of inner cuts. Unfortunately, this oversight resulted in the creation of two pin holes that were larger than intended during CNC milling at the BU EPIC lab. Despite this setback, I am  overall satisfied with the project, highlighting the comprehensive learning experience that encompassed measurement, sketching, CAD modeling, and CNC milling processes at BU EPIC.

High-Precision Low Dexterity Helping Hand "Armagetit"

Project 1 of ME360 asked us to chose a topic and build a device that moved in 2.5 degrees of freedom using stepper motors, aluminum bars, 3D printed parts
For our project, our group chose to build a helping hand tool for people with low dexterity that allowed high precision operations
Say someone is unable to precisely bring a tool to a workpiece? That is where our project applies! However, Armagetit can be used for anyone wishing for high tool precision
The user simply inputs x and y coordinates to move the desired tool to the work piece in the 5in x10in area, and then a z angle to rotate the lever arm down
For parts and wiring, we used three stepper motors, an Arduino Board, 3D printed parts, and a Soldering Iron. Two of the motors were the X and Y movement, and on the horizontal top bar was the third Z direction motor. The .5DOF motor was a strong servo which moved the designed lever arm that held the tool that is working on the desired application. Simple Gcode commands were sent to Repetier Host to control all four motors (shown below)
For our presentation, we chose to use a soldering iron to move to a metal workpiece to solder and unsolder two wires. The only user input were coordinates, and holding the wire to the solder joint. Armagetit moved to the correct locations, applied the iron, lifted to allow for joint cooling, and then applied the iron again to unsolder each wire
Overall, the project was a success. However, if brought into daily use I would purchase a new Arduino board since the old board used from the lab caused us strange issues we couldn't explain. These issues were easily solved with restarting the board, however this is unacceptable for daily use. Also, I would introduce a larger work area and a stronger way of holding the workpiece in one location

Car with Motor Speed Control

Project 2 of ME360 consisted of designing a simple car that used a motor with a speed controller.
The constraints of the project were that the car must travel 5 feet exactly there and back and it must accelerate and decelerate in a way to balance a one foot upright beam.
Another challenge was that we were competing with the entire class to see which car could achieve the fastest time.
Below is my designed simulation and motion analysis of the car project, designed to match the real-life project as much as possible
In my Solidworks assembly, I am only using the bare-bone necessities of the project to analyze and calculate at which acceleration the bar is projected to tip over, and based on this value, what is the fastest possible time simulated for our design
With this, any real-life found time which is slower than the simulation can be blamed on the added wheels to the car
During the simulation, we found the maximum theoretical acceleration of the cart before the bar tips by simulating a constantly accelerating cart util a moment force being measured by Solidworks reaches a calculated value found for the bar to tip over (figures below)
We also implemented a velocity curve (shown below) in the simulation to successfully move and accelerate the cart with the bar along the project parameters, and then implemented this into Arduino code to control the cart with the simple code provided during lecture
My simulation time was just above 6 seconds, and our approximate time in real-life was just over 10 seconds
Based on our manufacturing quality, we noticed slight slipping of the wheels we designed and even though we increased the friction of the wheel with tape, the experimental time was still slower than the simulation
Originally, we wanted to use purchased wheels provided by Professor Gutierrez, however the supply ran out before we could use them
For future cars, if we wanted to match the simulation time we would manufacture thicker and smaller wheels with rubber, and would assemble them with a stopper at the end of the axel for a better fit and roll
