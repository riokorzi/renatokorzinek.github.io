Unsteady Fluid Mechanics and Acoustics Laboratory

Student Researcher under Professor Sheryl Grace working with Raytheon Technologies Research Center(RTRC) and NASA Undergraduate Leadership Initiative (ULI) in projects focusing on reducing propellor and turbofan acoustic noise from turbulent flow in UAVs and Turbofan Engines. The focus being a quieter, greener future as urban landscapes are introduced to more air traffic in the future.

Data Analysis Presentation Research MATLAB Python Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) Airfoil Design

Project Overview

From the Summer of 2023 to the Spring of 2025 I was a student researcher with Professor Sheryl Grace at the Unsteady Fluid Mechanics and Acoustics Laboratory. My research began with literature review and analysis of wind tunnel data on turbofan engine blades from Raytheon Technologies Research Center (RTRC). With this data I analyzed and plotted sources of noise in the turbulent airflow section of the turbofan engine, specifically as this flow interacted with the stator vane. On this project I developed advanced MATLAB skills and worked closely with PhD student Nuo Li. This work familiarized me with current methodologies for mitigating broadband noise and improving blade efficiency in turbulent wake conditions. Over the past year and a half, I moved to a NASA ULI project that focused on UAV rotor blades in turbulent airflows for urban air mobility and delivery drones. On this project I learned how to use computational fluid dynamics (CFD) tools such as OpenFOAM, XROTOR, PointWise, Overflow, and CHARM. I have particularly focused on the rotor blade from Joby Aviation in Santa Cruz, CA, an electric vertical takeoff and landing (eVTOL) startup focused on unmanned air taxi services. On this project I conducted many CFD simulations to model flow behavior and noise generation in various propulsion configurations. Additionally, I used MATLAB and Python to post-process and visualize large datasets created by the CFD simulations. My goal has been the generation of airfoil tables as a strong and reliable baseline dataset for future research use. With this work I solved and debugged many issues found in mesh generation and turbulence calculations. I was closely mentored by PhD student Adam Rozman to ultimately create the airfoil tables for different sections of the Joby rotor. From this work, I came to draw actionable insights on aerodynamic and acoustic performance.

Key deliverables included validated performance analysis of rotor geometries and simulation-based models applicable to unmanned aerial systems and urban air mobility vehicles. Regular documentation and reporting of my progress through presentations and updates to Professor Grace and other students on the project have been essential to refining methodologies and aligning research with practical team research and industry goals.

Technical Achievements

• Learned advanced MATLAB strategies and techniques
• Learned and utilized CFD programs and mesh generation (OpenFOAM, Xfoil, Overflow, Charm, and Pointwise)
• Aided in the turbelent flow analysis for acoustics in turbofan engines
• Ran CFD on many propellor airfoils and compared various CFD codes
• Successfully troubleshot mesh generation issues and variations for validating turbelent flow for the Joby airfoil
• Analyzed various turbelence models and compared results
• Delivered reliable baseline airfoil table for a suite of spans and mach numbers for the Joby proprietary airfoil for future research use

Results & Impact

This hands-on research experience has significantly strengthened my technical capabilities in MATLAB, Python, CFD, acoustic modeling, and aerospace systems analysis. It also played a monumental role in helping me secure a high-level internship at Joby Aviation, where I applied my research skills to real-world eVTOL technologies as a Rotordynamics Intern out in California in the summer of 2024. The knowledge gained from this work not only advanced my academic and professional trajectory but also deepened my understanding of rotodynamic systems and developing quieter, cleaner, and more efficient aerospace systems. I wish to thank Professor Grace for this wonderful opportunity I took part in over the past couple years and wish to relay the true influence and importance it has had on my career. PEM Fuel Cell Design and Hydrogen Production

Led senior deisgn team toward the design and manufacturing of a custom hydrogen fuel cell using Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) technology. Focused on producing hydrogen from DI water, using new materials, optimizing flow channels, testing hydrogen sensors, and benchmarking performance.

CAD Design 3D Printing Manufacturing Electrochemistry MATLAB PEM Fuel Cell Design Hydrogen Testing

Project Overview

This project focuses on the design and development of a Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) electrolysis system capable of producing green hydrogen at a competitive cost, with scalability for widespread adoption. The objective was to lay the groundwork for a robust design that can be scaled from a single consumer to mass market and validate basic hydrogen generation capabilities through preliminary experiments.

To validate initial concepts, our team acquired an affordable PEM cell for testing. The tests confirmed the cell's ability to produce hydrogen, but also revealed challenges in maintaining continuous hydrogen generation, such as inconsistent water flow and gas separation issues. These findings highlighted the need for additional testing and optimization in preparation for the testing of the cell the team manufactured. After the initial tests, the team integrated pumps to regulate water flow and hydrogen output, and invested in a hydrogen sensor to improve test result accuracy. This phase of testing provided critical insights that informed the design and evaluation of the manufactured PEM system.

Building on the insights from initial testing, our team proceeded to design a PEM electrolyzer using high-performance materials such as Nafion N117 membranes for optimal proton conductivity, titanium mesh for efficient gas distribution, and aluminum 6061-T6 compression plates for lightweight structural support. This design emphasizes modularity, allowing for scalability across residential, industrial, and transportation applications. By focusing on these key design features, the team aimed to create a system that is both cost-effective and adaptable for a range of practical applications. The final deliverables of our work include:
• A comprehensive system design, including CAD models and specifications, ready for construction and testing.
• Preliminary testing data from a commercially available PEM cell, identifying both the feasibility and limitations of current methods.
• A bill of materials, emphasizing cost-effective, high-performance components to support the project’s scalability and affordability goals.
• A fully operational PEM cell capable of producing hydrogen without direct emissions
• Final testing data for the manufactured PEM cell and purchased PEM cell, enabling comparison of results and validation of the team's design decisions.

Technical Achievements

• Researched various materials and the Electrochemistry neccesary for hydrogen production
• Designed custom bipolar plates and aluminum housing using Solidworks
• Designed new proton exchange membrane layout by reverse engineering prior PEM cells
• Optimized channel geometry for uniform reactant distribution and water management
• Fabricated testing stand and easily portable setup
• Integrated hydrogen detection sensors for data collection
• Conducted extensive performance testing under various operating conditions
• Successfully produced Hydrogen with our own designed PEM cell
• Benchmarked our results against commercial PEM fuel cell units

Results & Impact

Throughout the course of this project, our Team 115 successfully designed, manufactured, and tested a custom Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) electrolysis cell for hydrogen production. The team’s work this semester built on last year’s purely research-focused efforts, moving toward testing and validation of hydrogen production techniques with a fully functioning physical prototype.

Starting with market research and material selection, the team prioritized affordability, durability, and efficiency in choosing components such as Nafion N117 membranes, titanium mesh, and aluminum compression plates. Manufacturing included custom waterjet-cut insulation layers, gasket seals, and machined housing elements to ensure proper assembly tightness and spacing, as well as effective hydrogen production. From reverse engineering the commercially purchased PEM cell the team learned key lessons and made critical improvements to our own design, particularly with membrane layering and compression.

For the second semester, testing was the central component of the project. After overcoming early challenges related to water flow and gas collection, the team developed a professional-grade test stand, implemented vacuum pumps for water recirculation, and employed hydrogen sensors to quantify hydrogen production. Comparative testing showed that although our manufactured PEM cell produced hydrogen at a slower rate than the purchased prototype, it operated more efficiently, validated our design approach, and pointed out improvements that could be made for future project work. The manufactured cell also required higher operating voltage and benefited from improved sealing techniques to mitigate issues like membrane leakage and electrical shorting.

Ultimately, the purchased PEM cell achieved faster hydrogen production in a closed volume, reaching steady-state conditions within approximately 125 seconds, while the manufactured PEM cell also demonstrated continuous Hydrogen production but at a slower rate. This result highlights the challenges of designing and optimizing a custom PEM electrolysis cell but also demonstrates the team's success in achieving a functional, working cell.

Going forward, further improvements could include using finer titanium meshes, refining membrane compression, and enhancing gasket seals to boost hydrogen production rates. Overall, this project provided valuable hands-on experience in electrochemical system design, manufacturing, testing, and validation. From this project, a strong foundation for future advancements in scalable, sustainable hydrogen production technologies is laid.

Thank you Team 115!

Second-Order Dynamic System Analysis in a Weakly Nonlinear Oscillator

Advanced signal processing and system identification using linear potentiometer measurements to analyze dynamic behavior and validate theoretical models.

Signal Processing MATLAB/Simulink Data Analysis Control Theory Dynamic 2nd Order Systems Uncertainty Analysis Nonlinear Oscillators Instrument Calibration

Project Overview

In ME310 Measurement and Instrumentation in the Spring of 2025 I was faced with the project that focused on the experimental characterization and theoretical modeling of a second-order dynamic system. Using precision instrumentation and advanced signal processing techniques, we achieved exceptional correlation between experimental data and mathematical models. In this experiment we analyzed a 2nd Order System in a scotch yoke mechanism with an unknown spring and oscillating mass. The team applied sinusoidal and step inputs to determine driving frequencies and dampening ratios and collected data via LabView. After analyzing data in Matlab we were able to compare our empirical results with theoretical ones and found that our signal processing techniques and control theories were correct and accurate, since they were directly correlated to theory.

Methodology

• Implemented high-precision linear potentiometer for displacement measurement
• Developed custom signal conditioning and filtering algorithms
• Applied system identification techniques to extract system parameters
• Validated results through frequency domain analysis and step response testing

Key Findings

In this project, we successfully identified the natural frequency, damping ratio, and system gain with high accuracy in the scotch-yoke machanism. The methodology developed for this 2nd Order dynamic system can be applied to various mechanical and aerospace systems for real-time monitoring and control applications, as it is commonly found.

High-Precision Low Dexterity Helping Hand "Armagetit"

Project 1 of ME360 asked us to chose a topic and build a device that moved in 2.5 degrees of freedom using stepper motors, aluminum bars, 3D printed parts
For our project, our group chose to build a helping hand tool for people with low dexterity that allowed high precision operations
Say someone is unable to precisely bring a tool to a workpiece? That is where our project applies! However, Armagetit can be used for anyone wishing for high tool precision
The user simply inputs x and y coordinates to move the desired tool to the work piece in the 5in x10in area, and then a z angle to rotate the lever arm down
For parts and wiring, we used three stepper motors, an Arduino Board, 3D printed parts, and a Soldering Iron. Two of the motors were the X and Y movement, and on the horizontal top bar was the third Z direction motor. The .5DOF motor was a strong servo which moved the designed lever arm that held the tool that is working on the desired application. Simple Gcode commands were sent to Repetier Host to control all four motors (shown below)
For our presentation, we chose to use a soldering iron to move to a metal workpiece to solder and unsolder two wires. The only user input were coordinates, and holding the wire to the solder joint. Armagetit moved to the correct locations, applied the iron, lifted to allow for joint cooling, and then applied the iron again to unsolder each wire
Overall, the project was a success. However, if brought into daily use I would purchase a new Arduino board since the old board used from the lab caused us strange issues we couldn't explain. These issues were easily solved with restarting the board, however this is unacceptable for daily use. Also, I would introduce a larger work area and a stronger way of holding the workpiece in one location

Car with Motor Speed Control

Project 2 of ME360 consisted of designing a simple car that used a motor with a speed controller.
The constraints of the project were that the car must travel 5 feet exactly there and back and it must accelerate and decelerate in a way to balance a one foot upright beam.
Another challenge was that we were competing with the entire class to see which car could achieve the fastest time.
Below is my designed simulation and motion analysis of the car project, designed to match the real-life project as much as possible
In my Solidworks assembly, I am only using the bare-bone necessities of the project to analyze and calculate at which acceleration the bar is projected to tip over, and based on this value, what is the fastest possible time simulated for our design
With this, any real-life found time which is slower than the simulation can be blamed on the added wheels to the car
During the simulation, we found the maximum theoretical acceleration of the cart before the bar tips by simulating a constantly accelerating cart util a moment force being measured by Solidworks reaches a calculated value found for the bar to tip over (figures below)
We also implemented a velocity curve (shown below) in the simulation to successfully move and accelerate the cart with the bar along the project parameters, and then implemented this into Arduino code to control the cart with the simple code provided during lecture
My simulation time was just above 6 seconds, and our approximate time in real-life was just over 10 seconds
Based on our manufacturing quality, we noticed slight slipping of the wheels we designed and even though we increased the friction of the wheel with tape, the experimental time was still slower than the simulation
Originally, we wanted to use purchased wheels provided by Professor Gutierrez, however the supply ran out before we could use them
For future cars, if we wanted to match the simulation time we would manufacture thicker and smaller wheels with rubber, and would assemble them with a stopper at the end of the axel for a better fit and roll
